Featured Research

MSc Dissertation: Decentering, Mindfulness & Rumination

My dissertation asked a question I found both scientifically and personally compelling: how does mindfulness relate to repetitive negative thinking — and what process might explain that link? I focused on three constructs: mindfulness, decentering (the ability to step back from thoughts and view them as temporary mental events rather than facts), and rumination.

Using a cross-sectional online survey distributed through Qualtrics and Prolific, I recruited 310 participants and, after data cleaning, worked with a final sample of 0 UK-based young adults aged 18–30. Each completed validated measures of mindfulness, decentering and rumination, which I analysed using correlation and mediation models.

Mindfulness was positively associated with decentering, and decentering was associated with lower rumination — though mindfulness itself showed a small positive association with rumination in this sample. The mediation analysis pointed to decentering as the key mechanism: mindfulness related to lower rumination specifically through the ability to step back from thoughts, not simply through noticing them more.

The project reinforced something I keep returning to in my work: awareness alone isn't always enough — how we relate to our thoughts may matter just as much as whether we notice them. It sharpened my skills in research design, ethics, survey development, participant recruitment, data cleaning, statistical analysis and communicating findings clearly. I'm grateful to the University of Exeter Psychology faculty for funding the participant recruitment that made this research possible.

Decentering carries mindfulness's effect: more mindfulness related to more decentering, which in turn related to lower rumination.

Featured UX Case Study

LIMELIGHT: Designing for Perceived Safety in Bangkok

As part of a design-thinking research project, my partner and I set out to understand why tourists felt unsafe walking around Soi Nana, a nightlife district in Bangkok — even though it has a reputation as a vibrant, welcoming area. We combined seven in-depth interviews, four ad-hoc street interviews, direct observation across two field visits, and official crime-report data that we requested directly from the local police station.

The data told two different stories: residents felt safe, citing close-knit community ties, while every tourist we spoke to felt unsafe at night — despite crime statistics showing the area was, in reality, low-risk. That contradiction became the heart of the project. We reframed the brief from "reduce crime" to "reduce perceived threat" — a problem about feelings and environment, not actual danger.

From ten brainstormed concepts — including a police statue, a 10pm curfew and a "walking game" — we ran a "worst ideas" exercise to extract lessons, then used a complexity/impact matrix to land on LIMELIGHT: a website that turns nearby users into an informal safety network, with an emergency alert button, live status updates, and route guidance to the nearest open cafe or bar.

I designed the interface in Adobe XD, from low-fidelity wireframes through to a high-fidelity UI with a deliberately chosen colour palette, then ran usability testing with five participants. Beyond the standard System Usability Scale, I added a twist suited to an emergency product: timing how quickly users could activate the alert under simulated urgency.

Featured Project

Nang Lerng Immersive Exhibition: Building for Handover

As Project Manager, I led a 30-person team across five departments — Marketing, Media Production, Design, Logistics and Business Partnerships — through an 8-phase, 7-month journey (research, recruitment, team-building, planning, promotion, design, pre-execution and execution) to deliver a 7-day, 11-programme exhibition celebrating Nang Loeng, one of Bangkok's oldest communities.

Rather than relying on existing activities, we designed and launched four new revenue-generating experiences from scratch: a food-tasting trail, a food-hunting "treasure hunt" across 17 partner cafes and restaurants plus 2 hostels, guided heritage walks, and craft workshops. Together they generated roughly 29,250 THB, reinvested directly into the community — with the food-hunting activity alone reaching 458 redemptions.

On the media side, our social following grew to 4,186, with 230.6K reach (+848%) and 88.2K engagement (+1234%) over 90 days — one Reel alone reached 86K accounts with 94K plays. We contacted 63 media outlets and secured coverage from 18, including the Bangkok Post, Time Out, The Standard and Urban Creature.

The part I'm proudest of is what happened after the exhibition ended: rather than just running an event, we designed for handover. We transferred the social media accounts, contact databases, partnership frameworks and ran capacity-building sessions so the Nang Loeng community could continue independently. Along the way, I learned that authority doesn't guarantee leadership — legitimacy, built through relationships and shared goals, is what actually makes cross-functional collaboration work, especially when navigating the internal politics of multiple community leaders who didn't always see eye to eye.
